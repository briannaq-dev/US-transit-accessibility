Introduction

Understanding transit accessibility in the United States is essential for evaluating how transportation systems shape economic opportunity, mobility, and quality of life across metropolitan regions. Public transit is a critical connector between residents and employment centers, yet the degree to which people can reliable reach jobs varies widely from city to city. Transit accessibility measures how many job opportunities an individual can reach within a reasonable travel time–in this case, within 45 minutes via transit and/or walking–and serves as a meaningful indicator of urban infrastructure performance, workforce inclusivity, and regional equity.

Monitoring differences in transit accessibility provides insights into broader social and economic outcomes, such as workers’ ability to reach stable employment, the effectiveness of local transportation investments, and the extend to which cities support upward mobility. High levels of job accessibility often reflect efficient transit networks, compact urban design, and equitable transportation planning. These factors can strengthen regional labor markets and support long-term economic growth. Conversely, low accessibility may signal geographic isolation, underinvestment in transit services, or disparities that affect low-income communities and essential workers.

Transit accessibility also intersects with socioeconomic factors such as income, housing, and spatial inequality. Metro areas with limited job access via transit may experience increased financial strain on households, reduced employment stability, and greater dependence on personal vehicles, which can burden disadvantaged communities. Examining how these disparities helps identify which regions foster inclusive mobility and which are falling behind in providing equitable access to opportunities.

To learn more about transit accessibility and national mobility patterns, additional information can be found through organizations such as the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency and the Bureau of Transportation Statistics.

Top 10 Metro Areas for Job Accessibility via Public Transit

This graph introduces the overall landscape of transit accessibility by highlighting the top ten metro areas with the highest percentage of jobs reachable within a forty-five-minute transit commute.

The results show that many of the strongest performers are smaller or mid-sized regions such as Corvallis, Missoula, and Butte Silver Bow. These areas demonstrate that high job accessibility often emerges when residential density, employment locations, and transit routes are closely aligned. In these regions, the physical distance between where people live and where jobs are located is short enough for transit to function efficiently, which helps explain why they rise to the top of the ranking.

This graph establishes a foundation for understanding how local geography and urban structure influence access to employment.

Map View of Job Accessibility via Public Transit

Knowing Corvallis, OR had the highest average percentage of jobs reachable through public transit, we wanted to understand the data in the larger picture by creating a choropleth map.

The choropleth map of Oregon shows a more fragmented distribution of transit accessibility across the state. Only a few concentrated areas around Portland, Salem, and Eugene appear in the highest accessibility category, while the majority of block groups fall into the lowest tier. This pattern reflects Oregon’s more rural and dispersed development structure, where job centers and residential areas are spread farther apart and transit coverage is less continuous. Even in regions with stronger service, high accessibility is limited to isolated pockets rather than forming a broad and cohesive network. These characteristics help explain why most Oregon metropolitan areas do not rank highly in overall job accessibility, despite some urban centers providing well-connected transit corridors.

In contrast, the map of Massachusetts displays much wider and more continuous areas of high transit accessibility, particularly in and around Greater Boston and along the major transit corridors that extend outward from the city. Large regions of the state fall into the upper accessibility categories, indicating a denser and more interconnected transit network. Yet, despite this strong spatial coverage, Massachusetts does not appear among the top metro areas for jobs reachable within forty five minutes.

Massachusetts also operates on a fare based transit system, while Corvallis provides free public transit, which lowers barriers to use and encourages higher ridership. As a result, metro areas like Corvallis, where jobs, residents, and free transit services are tightly concentrated, can achieve higher overall job accessibility than larger regions with broader but more dispersed networks such as Massachusetts.

Comparing largest disparities in job accessibility via public transit by wage

The side by side bar chart explores socioeconomic issues by comparing job accessibility for low wage and medium wage workers in the metro areas with the largest differences.

In many of the regions shown in the chart, low wage workers actually have access to a higher percentage of jobs within a forty five minute transit commute when compared to medium wage workers. Cities such as Bloomington, Blacksburg, Fayetteville, Ann Arbor, and Champaign Urbana all show this pattern, which suggests that jobs available to lower wage workers may be located closer to dense housing or situated along transit routes with more frequent service. Only a few metro areas, including Salina, Springfield, and Reno Sparks, show the more common pattern where medium wage workers have an advantage.

This variation demonstrates that transit accessibility does not follow a single national trend and instead depends on how each region organizes its job locations and transit network. The chart highlights that some metro areas may be supporting mobility for low wage workers more effectively than expected, while others continue to show gaps that affect both equity and opportunity.
